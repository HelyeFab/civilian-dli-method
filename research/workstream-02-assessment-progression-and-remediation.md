# Workstream 02 — Assessment, Progression, and Remediation

**Status:** Initial evidence synthesis  
**Research stream:** Independent assistant investigation  
**Last updated:** 2026-07-19

## Research question

How does DLIFLC measure learner progress, distinguish course performance from real-world proficiency, identify learners at risk, and intervene before final graduation testing?

## Executive finding

The evidence supports a layered control system rather than a single final examination:

1. **Course-based assessment** checks whether learners are keeping pace with the resident curriculum.
2. **In-Course Proficiency Tests (ICPTs)** estimate whether learners are developing broader proficiency at key programme stages.
3. **Final resident-course examinations** assess course outcomes.
4. **The Defense Language Proficiency Test (DLPT)** measures listening and reading against the Interagency Language Roundtable (ILR) scale.
5. **The Oral Proficiency Interview (OPI)** measures spontaneous, unrehearsed speaking.
6. **Special Assistance (SA)** and related targeted interventions are used for learners whose performance indicates elevated risk.
7. **Recycling and graduation-readiness interventions** can be selected using test data and individual needs.

This architecture reduces the risk that a learner can succeed merely by memorising a textbook or rehearsing predictable classroom answers. The endpoint is functional proficiency, while the internal assessments provide earlier diagnostic signals.

## 1. The external proficiency framework

DLIFLC states that its Language Proficiency Assessment Directorate designs, validates, implements, and monitors the DLPT, as well as performance tests and final examinations for resident programmes.

The DLPT measures listening and reading comprehension using the ILR proficiency scale. The OPI programme is maintained by DLIFLC's Proficiency Standards Division.

The ILR scale is functional and ordinal. It describes what a learner can do with the language, not which textbook chapters have been completed. Its base levels run from 0 to 5, with plus levels between most base levels.

### Evidence classification

- **Verified:** DLPT listening and reading are aligned to ILR proficiency descriptions.
- **Verified:** DLIFLC separately develops resident performance tests and final examinations.
- **Verified:** OPI tester training and quality maintenance form a dedicated institutional function.
- **Strongly supported inference:** DLIFLC intentionally separates curriculum attainment from global proficiency measurement.

## 2. What the DLPT and OPI measure

### DLPT

The DLPT is intended to measure listening and reading proficiency rather than mastery of one course's internal content. DLIFLC describes the DLPT and OPI as scientifically validated tools used across the Department of Defense.

A learner therefore needs to process unfamiliar material at the functional level represented by the target ILR rating. The exact item-selection and security procedures are not public and should not be reconstructed speculatively.

### OPI

The OPI evaluates spontaneous, unrehearsed spoken language. ACTFL's official description states that the interview considers comprehensibility, content and context, text type, vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation, fluency, pragmatic competence, and sociolinguistic competence holistically.

The OPI is important to this reconstruction because it strongly discourages a curriculum built around memorised dialogues alone. Rehearsed speech cannot reliably demonstrate global speaking proficiency, and interviewers adapt prompts to identify the speaker's functional ceiling.

### Civilian implication

A civilian system inspired by DLI should not rely exclusively on lesson quizzes. It needs two different assessment families:

- **performance checks:** Did the learner master the material taught this week?
- **proficiency probes:** Can the learner understand or produce unfamiliar language at the intended functional level?

These tests should not be identical.

## 3. In-course proficiency testing

A 2024 DLIFLC action-research study describes an eight-week Arabic recycling programme and compares learner scores across:

- ICPT 301;
- ICPT 302;
- DLPT;
- OPI.

This is direct evidence that at least some programmes use staged in-course proficiency testing before the final external-style assessments. The study also reports that instructors selected materials and individualised instruction using analysis of learner needs and test performance.

### What this establishes

- **Verified within the cited Arabic context:** ICPT results can be used as programme-stage indicators.
- **Verified within the cited intervention:** results from multiple assessments informed material selection and individualised instruction.
- **Strongly supported:** assessment data are not only used for grading; they can drive instructional changes.
- **Not yet verified institution-wide:** the precise timing, number, format, and consequences of ICPTs across every language school.

### Source

- [Wilkins — Recycling Lower-Level Concepts in Semester III to Build Language Proficiency](https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/12)

## 4. Special Assistance as targeted intervention

DLIFLC faculty describe Special Assistance as a programme providing targeted academic support for learners considered at risk.

The most recent available faculty analysis reports that students perceive SA as most valuable when it is:

- tailored to their actual weaknesses;
- rich in learning strategies;
- delivered in a supportive environment;
- connected to learner autonomy and confidence.

Students perceive it as less effective when it merely repeats normal classroom tasks or feels punitive.

This is a major design insight. Remediation is not simply “more hours of the same lesson.” Effective remediation changes the instructional treatment.

### Reconstructed SA cycle

The following is an evidence-based reconstruction, not an official universal procedure:

1. A learner's classroom performance or proficiency indicator reveals a weakness.
2. The weakness is diagnosed at a finer level: decoding, vocabulary retrieval, grammar processing, reading rate, listening segmentation, speaking organisation, motivation, or study strategy.
3. A targeted intervention is assigned.
4. The learner practises with different scaffolding or strategy instruction.
5. Progress is checked against the original weakness.
6. The intervention is adjusted, continued, or concluded.

### Evidence status

- **Verified:** SA is used for targeted support of at-risk students.
- **Verified from student-perspective research:** tailoring, strategy training, and a non-punitive climate are valued.
- **Strongly supported inference:** diagnosis should precede remediation.
- **Unverified:** one mandatory institute-wide SA workflow with identical entry and exit rules.

### Source

- [Kalyanov — Maximizing the Effectiveness of Special Assistance](https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/47)

## 5. Recycling as assessment-led intervention

The Arabic action-research study used an eight-week intervention to recycle lower-level material during a later semester. It combined:

- core curriculum;
- authentic listening and reading materials;
- speaking practice;
- comprehensive review;
- individualised instruction based on test performance and learner needs.

The reported results suggest that recycling foundational material can support later proficiency development and graduation readiness.

The important mechanism is not simple repetition. It is **selective retrieval of earlier knowledge inside more advanced tasks**.

### Civilian translation

A civilian DLI-style programme should maintain a weakness register. When later performance reveals a foundational gap, the system should reintroduce the relevant lower-level material inside current-level listening, reading, and speaking tasks.

Example:

- current task: discuss a news report in Japanese;
- detected weakness: unreliable past-tense and causal linking;
- remediation: recycle those structures through short listening, reconstruction, and spontaneous retelling tasks using the current topic.

This preserves relevance while repairing the foundation.

## 6. Graduation standards: what is and is not established

Public materials clearly establish that the DLPT and OPI are central final proficiency measures. They do not, from the sources reviewed in this workstream, establish one immutable graduation score for every language, service, occupation, and historical period.

Numbers such as `2/2/1+`, `2/2/2`, or higher are frequently discussed in military-language contexts, but they must not be presented as a single universal DLIFLC rule without a current authoritative policy source defining:

- language category;
- service branch;
- occupational specialty;
- course type;
- date of policy;
- waiver rules.

### Current claim status

> DLIFLC graduates are evaluated using DLPT listening/reading and OPI speaking results.

**Status:** Verified.

> Every Basic Course student must achieve one identical L/R/S score combination.

**Status:** Unverified and likely context-dependent.

## 7. Assessment shapes the curriculum

The assessment model creates several predictable pressures on instruction:

### Unfamiliar input

Because proficiency tests use functional ability rather than textbook recall, learners require practice with unfamiliar listening and reading material.

### Spontaneous output

Because the OPI evaluates unrehearsed speech, learners require frequent unscripted production beyond memorised dialogues.

### Broad topic coverage

Official OPI guidance recommends discussion beyond a textbook's narrow topic range, including unpredictable and real-world topics.

### Integrated review

Because later tests expose foundational weaknesses, old material must remain active rather than disappearing after a unit test.

### Diagnostic granularity

A low score alone is insufficient. Effective remediation requires identifying why the learner failed: vocabulary, parsing, speed, discourse organisation, cultural knowledge, anxiety, or strategy use.

## 8. Preliminary civilian assessment architecture

The following model is an adaptation, not a claim about exact DLI procedure.

### Daily

- brief retrieval check;
- listening or reading micro-probe;
- one spontaneous production sample;
- error tagging by skill and cause.

### Weekly

- curriculum performance test;
- cumulative old-material section;
- unfamiliar input section;
- short unrehearsed speaking task;
- learner self-assessment.

### Every four to six weeks

- proficiency probe using unseen material;
- recorded speaking interview;
- reading and listening tasks mapped to functional descriptors;
- review of longitudinal weaknesses;
- intervention decision.

### Intervention trigger

Trigger targeted assistance when one of the following persists:

- repeated weakness across three or more sessions;
- falling comprehension despite adequate vocabulary knowledge;
- inability to transfer known grammar to spontaneous production;
- major mismatch between course scores and unseen-material performance;
- sustained decline in speed, accuracy, confidence, or attendance.

### Remediation rule

Do not prescribe “study more.” Prescribe a specific treatment linked to a diagnosed bottleneck.

## 9. Working model

The available evidence supports this assessment loop:

> Teach → measure course performance → probe global proficiency → diagnose the bottleneck → assign targeted assistance → recycle foundations in current-level tasks → reassess → adjust progression.

This is currently classified as **strongly supported reconstruction**, not a quoted DLIFLC doctrine.
