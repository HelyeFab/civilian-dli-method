# Workstream 04 — Civilian Routine Extraction for Japanese

## Research objective

This workstream changes the project’s operational question.

The goal is no longer merely to describe how DLIFLC works as an institution. The goal is to identify which parts of its instructional architecture can be transferred into one civilian learner’s daily Japanese routine.

The guiding question is:

> What can a self-directed Japanese learner realistically steal from DLI without copying its military environment, staffing levels, or total study hours?

This document separates:

1. directly supported DLI practices;
2. cautious instructional inferences;
3. proposed civilian adaptations;
4. practices that should not yet be claimed as DLI-derived.

---

## 1. What is directly established

### 1.1 Intensity and regularity

DLIFLC states that resident students normally study their target language for seven classroom hours per day, five days per week, followed by two to three hours of homework each night.

Evidence status: **Verified**

Source:

- https://www.dliflc.edu/about/student-life/
- https://www.dliflc.edu/about/

This does not prove that hours alone cause proficiency. It does establish that language contact is frequent, sustained, and difficult to postpone.

### 1.2 Multiple skill domains are trained

Public DLIFLC resources distinguish beginner sound-and-script training, listening, reading, vocabulary, grammar, cultural knowledge, assessment, and advanced authentic-material work.

Evidence status: **Verified**

Sources:

- https://www.dliflc.edu/search/
- https://gloss.dliflc.edu/

### 1.3 Listening is treated as a trainable processing skill

Recent DLIFLC faculty research explicitly argues against treating listening difficulty as merely a vocabulary deficit. It describes structured work on chunking, syntactic parsing, clause recognition, syntactic mapping, and real-time processing.

Evidence status: **Strongly supported**

Sources:

- https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/45
- https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/48

### 1.4 Vocabulary is more than translation recall

DLIFLC faculty publications describe knowing a word as involving meaning, written form, phonological form, syntactic category, connotation, and use. Vocabulary-strategy training combines digital and traditional tools and asks learners to develop an explicit learning algorithm.

Evidence status: **Strongly supported**

Sources:

- https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/22
- https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/26

### 1.5 Remediation should be diagnostic, not punitive

DLIFLC’s Special Assistance literature reports that learners find support most valuable when it is tailored, strategy-rich, and supportive. Repeating ordinary classroom work or framing extra help as punishment is perceived as less effective.

Evidence status: **Strongly supported**

Source:

- https://www.dliflc.edu/ojs/dialog/article/view/47

### 1.6 Authentic Japanese material is publicly available by level and modality

GLOSS includes Japanese lessons searchable by proficiency level, modality, competence, topic, and subtopic.

Evidence status: **Verified**

Source:

- https://gloss.dliflc.edu/

## 2. The first transferable principle: copy architecture, not hours

A civilian learner should not simply divide DLI’s timetable by three and assume the result is equivalent.

DLI’s advantage includes:

- institutional accountability;
- trained instructors;
- a cohort;
- formal testing;
- immediate correction;
- curated materials;
- compulsory attendance;
- sustained exposure over many months.

Those conditions cannot be reproduced merely by doing more Anki cards.

The transferable element is the architecture:

1. daily contact;
2. cumulative retrieval;
3. explicit listening work;
4. controlled and spontaneous production;
5. unfamiliar input;
6. assessment;
7. diagnosis;
8. targeted correction;
9. continued recycling.

Evidence status for this complete sequence: **Inferred synthesis**

It is a reconstruction from multiple sources, not a published universal DLIFLC lesson formula.

## 3. What Emmanuel can steal now

## 3.1 A three-hour core routine

This is a proposed civilian adaptation, not an official DLI schedule.

### Block A — Retrieval before recognition: 25 minutes

Purpose:

- force active recall;
- reactivate old grammar and chunks;
- identify what is becoming inaccessible.

Activities:

1. Say yesterday’s five target sentences without looking.
2. Answer five previously studied questions aloud.
3. Produce two variations of each important pattern.
4. Write a brief error list only after attempting recall.

Rules:

- no rereading before retrieval;
- full sentences rather than isolated vocabulary;
- include material from several weeks ago, not only yesterday.

DLI-derived principle:

- cumulative performance and recycling.

Evidence status of exact activity design: **Civilian adaptation**

### Block B — Bottom-up listening: 35 minutes

Purpose:

- improve real-time decoding rather than merely testing comprehension.

Use one short Japanese audio segment, ideally 20–60 seconds.

Procedure:

1. Listen once without text and state the gist.
2. Listen again and mark phrase boundaries.
3. Identify predicates, particles, clause endings, and omitted subjects.
4. Transcribe only the sections that remain unclear.
5. Compare with the transcript.
6. Shadow the same passage in short chunks.
7. Give an oral summary without copying its wording.

DLI-derived principle:

- listening must train parsing, chunking, sound discrimination, and real-time syntactic processing.

Evidence status: **Strongly supported principle; adapted procedure**

### Block C — Structured language expansion: 35 minutes

Purpose:

- turn one grammar pattern or discourse function into usable language.

Procedure:

1. Select one pattern already encountered in meaningful input.
2. Build three personally relevant model sentences.
3. Transform each sentence by changing time, polarity, person, or context.
4. Ask and answer questions that force the pattern.
5. Contrast it with one nearby pattern that you commonly confuse.

Example progression:

- model: 日本へ行ったら、友達に会いたいです。
- negative condition;
- past consequence;
- question form;
- spontaneous personal answer.

DLI-derived principle:

- constrained production should precede freer production; grammar should be processed and used rather than merely recognised.

Evidence status: **Inferred from faculty grammar and classroom research; exact drill is adapted**

### Block D — Reading with topic development: 30 minutes

Purpose:

- develop reading ability and broader conceptual knowledge.

Use a short graded or authentic text at an achievable level.

Procedure:

1. Read once for the author’s main point.
2. Identify the function of each paragraph.
3. Extract no more than five high-value items.
4. Record each item with pronunciation, grammar behaviour, collocation, and one sentence from memory.
5. Explain the text aloud in Japanese.

DLI-derived principle:

- authentic or increasingly authentic material, topic breadth, and learning words as multidimensional items.

Evidence status: **Supported principle; civilian adaptation**

### Block E — Guided-to-free speaking: 30 minutes

Purpose:

- bridge rehearsed knowledge and spontaneous production.

Procedure:

1. Two minutes of controlled answers using today’s target pattern.
2. Five minutes of guided questions where the same function appears in varied contexts.
3. Five minutes of retelling or explanation.
4. Five minutes of an unrehearsed task.
5. Review the recording and identify only three priority errors.

Possible unrehearsed tasks:

- explain a decision;
- compare two travel options;
- summarise a news item;
- describe a problem and request a solution;
- defend an opinion;
- narrate an unexpected event.

DLI-derived principle:

- speaking should include performance tasks and unrehearsed output, not only pleasant conversation.

Evidence status: **Strong inference; adapted for self-study**

### Block F — Diagnostic repair: 25 minutes

Purpose:

- stop repeatedly practising what is already comfortable.

Procedure:

1. Review errors from listening, reading, and speaking.
2. Classify the main bottleneck:
   - sound discrimination;
   - parsing;
   - vocabulary access;
   - grammar selection;
   - conjugation speed;
   - discourse organisation;
   - pronunciation;
   - cultural or conceptual knowledge.
3. Choose one short repair exercise aimed only at that bottleneck.
4. Reperform the failed task.

DLI-derived principle:

- assistance should be tailored, strategy-rich, and followed by another performance opportunity.

Evidence status: **Strongly supported principle; adapted workflow**

## 3.2 A ninety-minute minimum routine

On lower-energy or medically demanding days, continuity matters more than pretending every day can support an intensive schedule.

Proposed minimum:

- 15 minutes cumulative retrieval;
- 25 minutes bottom-up listening;
- 20 minutes guided production;
- 20 minutes reading or authentic input;
- 10 minutes error diagnosis and repair.

Non-negotiable rule:

> A reduced day may be shorter, but it should not become a recognition-only day.

At least one activity must require spoken or written production from memory.

Evidence status: **Civilian adaptation**

## 4. Weekly control system

A DLI-inspired routine requires accountability outside the daily lesson.

### Weekly test 1 — Unseen listening

Use a short recording not studied previously.

Measure separately:

- gist;
- key facts;
- phrase-boundary recognition;
- verb and clause recognition;
- ability to summarise.

Do not add the recording to the normal study set until after the first attempt.

### Weekly test 2 — Recorded speaking

Complete one five-to-ten-minute unrehearsed task.

Track:

- pauses caused by retrieval failure;
- abandoned sentences;
- dependence on simple connectors;
- repeated grammar errors;
- pronunciation that affects comprehension;
- ability to organise an answer.

### Weekly test 3 — Cumulative retrieval

Sample material from:

- this week;
- two weeks ago;
- one month ago;
- an older weak area.

### Weekly review meeting with oneself

Answer:

1. What improved in observable performance?
2. What failed under time pressure?
3. Which weakness is now the priority?
4. Which comfortable activity should lose time next week?
5. What will be recycled rather than declared complete?

Evidence status for exact tests: **Civilian adaptation grounded in DLI’s layered assessment and remediation principles**

## 5. Rules to impose on the routine

These are the most important environmental constraints that a self-directed learner can reproduce.

### Rule 1 — Every day contains output

Watching Japanese content is not enough.

The learner must retrieve, speak, write, summarise, transform, or explain.

### Rule 2 — Old language remains active

No grammar point is considered finished merely because its textbook chapter ended.

Previously learned language must reappear under new topics and time pressure.

### Rule 3 — Listening practice is not only repeated listening

Each difficult passage must be diagnosed:

- Was the sound not heard?
- Was the word known but not recognised?
- Was the clause structure missed?
- Was processing too slow?
- Was the concept itself unfamiliar?

### Rule 4 — Conversation must have a training target

A tutor session should specify:

- target patterns;
- target chunks;
- discourse functions;
- previously learned items to reactivate;
- one spontaneous performance task.

This directly addresses the failure mode where the tutor speaks for most of the session and the learner participates only reactively.

### Rule 5 — Correction is selective but recorded

Not every error needs immediate interruption.

However, recurring errors must enter a weakness register and trigger later repair.

### Rule 6 — Recognition cannot count as mastery

A word or structure is not considered available until it can be:

- understood in unfamiliar input;
- recalled without prompting;
- used in a novel sentence;
- retrieved under modest time pressure.

### Rule 7 — The study plan changes in response to evidence

Time allocation should follow demonstrated weakness, not habit or preference.

Evidence status of these rules: **Mixed—some directly supported principles, some cautious synthesis**

## 6. What not to steal

### 6.1 Exhaustion as a badge of effectiveness

DLI’s workload is documented as extremely demanding. That does not establish that sleep loss or chronic overload improves learning.

A civilian adaptation should preserve consistency and difficulty without reproducing military pressure or burnout.

### 6.2 Raw study-hour targets

Seven classroom hours plus homework are part of a staffed institution. A solo learner cannot assume that ten unfocused hours produce the same value.

### 6.3 Vocabulary quantity without processing

Faculty research explicitly warns against overestimating vocabulary as the sole explanation for listening success.

### 6.4 Repeating failed exercises unchanged

Special Assistance evidence indicates that simply duplicating normal classroom tasks is perceived as less useful than tailored intervention.

### 6.5 Premature free conversation

Open conversation can expose weaknesses, but it does not automatically repair them. It should follow retrieval, controlled variation, and guided production.

Evidence status: **Combination of supported cautions and instructional inference**

---

## 7. Immediate implementation proposal

For the next four weeks, the learner should run the routine as an experiment rather than declare it a permanent method.

Track daily:

- minutes spent;
- active-output minutes;
- Japanese spoken without reading;
- unseen listening score;
- number of old chunks successfully retrieved;
- principal bottleneck;
- repair activity completed;
- whether the failed task was reperformed.

Track weekly:

- one recorded monologue or task;
- one unseen listening sample;
- one cumulative retrieval test;
- one revised priority for the next week.

Success should be judged by performance change, not by streaks or perceived effort alone.

## 8. Current confidence limits

The following claims are not yet established and require further research:

- the precise minute-by-minute structure of a typical Japanese DLI classroom;
- a universal DLI flashcard or spaced-repetition protocol;
- a standard daily proportion assigned to listening, speaking, reading, and writing;
- a universal instructor correction policy;
- a definitive list of behaviours distinguishing top graduates from average students;
- the precise out-of-class routines used by successful Japanese-programme students.

These must remain open research questions rather than be filled with plausible-sounding assumptions.
