# Workstream 01 — Daily Structure and Lesson Model

## Research question

What can be established, with credible evidence, about the structure of a normal DLIFLC student day and the internal logic of classroom instruction?

## Executive finding

The daily workload is well documented. DLIFLC students normally spend five days per week and seven hours per day in class, followed by approximately two to three hours of homework. Some historical programme descriptions report six to seven classroom hours, but the current official student-life page states seven.

The internal lesson recipe is not published as one universal institution-wide sequence. Evidence instead indicates a policy-governed instructional environment in which:

1. the target language is expected to be the primary classroom language;
2. teachers adapt instruction to proficiency, learner needs and language-specific constraints;
3. explicit strategy instruction is used for difficult skills such as listening and vocabulary;
4. production tasks are integrated with receptive-skill work;
5. recycling, remediation and special assistance are normal parts of the system;
6. English remains available when concept knowledge, clarity or cognitive load requires it.

Therefore, describing DLI as "pure immersion" is misleading.

---

## 1. Verified daily structure

### Classroom load

DLIFLC's current Student Life page states that students spend:

- five days each week in class;
- seven hours per day learning the target language;
- two to three hours per night on homework.

Military students also complete service-specific physical training, military tasks and administrative obligations outside the academic core.

### Historical consistency

Older DLIFLC features describe comparable schedules:

- Korean: six to seven hours in class and two to three hours of homework;
- institute-wide descriptions: up to seven classroom hours and three or more hours of homework.

The exact number may vary by date, school or programme, but the consistent institutional fact is sustained full-time intensive study.

### Claim status

**Verified institutional fact.**

### Sources

- DLIFLC, "Student Life — A day in the life of a DLIFLC student"
- DLIFLC, "Homework Korean Style"
- DLIFLC, "Master Sergeant loves mentoring, giving back"

## 2. What the evidence does not support

The reviewed official sources do not establish that every lesson follows a rigid sequence such as:

> vocabulary → grammar explanation → dialogue → drill → quiz

Such a sequence may occur in individual schools or courses, but it should not be presented as a universal DLI lesson architecture without stronger evidence.

Similarly, the evidence does not support the claim that students simply absorb the language through uninterrupted immersion. DLIFLC faculty publications describe explicit instruction in grammar, vocabulary-learning strategies, bottom-up listening, syntactic parsing, concept checking, reading fluency and remediation.

### Claim status

**Strongly supported correction of a common misconception.**

---

## 3. Target-language use

A DLIFLC Target Language Use Command Policy was issued in October 2022 and reinforced in July 2024. A 2024 study covering Arabic, Mandarin, Korean, Persian Farsi, Russian and Spanish found that teachers generally made the target language the primary classroom language.

Evidence base included:

- 20 classroom observations;
- 15 interviews with deans and chairs;
- 83 faculty survey responses;
- 222 student survey responses;
- 6,631 end-of-programme student evaluations.

Students rated the statement that instructors ensured the target language was primary in class at 3.73 out of 4.

However, the same study documented implementation challenges and the need for training. A later DLIFLC faculty reflection also described a case where English was deliberately used to explain a difficult unfamiliar concept.

### Interpretation

The most defensible model is **target-language-primary, not target-language-exclusive**.

### Claim status

**Verified for the six basic-course languages studied; likely broader as institutional policy, but not assumed identical in every classroom.**

## 4. Listening instruction

Recent DLIFLC action research shows that listening is treated as a skill requiring explicit processing instruction rather than only increased exposure.

### Six-step syntactic-processing approach

A Persian Farsi intervention progressed from identifying subject–verb units in short sentences toward processing complex and compound sentences with embedded verbs, clauses, prepositional phrases and lower-frequency vocabulary. Strategies included:

- chunking;
- syntactic mapping;
- identifying dependent and independent clauses;
- identifying verb mood;
- building real-time processing capacity.

The author reports improvement in DLPT listening outcomes among learners who had serious listening difficulties.

### Bottom-up listening lessons

A French action-research project placed bottom-up listening activities between an introductory activity and a production task. Students evaluated the lessons positively, although the author cautioned that bottom-up work should be supplemented with comprehension-focused activities.

### Interpretation

A likely DLIFLC lesson principle is:

> isolate a processing bottleneck, teach a strategy explicitly, practise it progressively, then reconnect it to meaning and production.

This is a reconstructed principle, not a published universal template.

### Claim status

**Strongly supported pedagogical mechanism; context-specific studies rather than institute-wide proof.**

## 5. Vocabulary instruction

DLIFLC faculty research rejects the idea that vocabulary learning is merely memorising word–translation pairs.

A three-week workshop with 18 introductory Russian learners:

- introduced multiple vocabulary-learning strategies;
- organised them into structured learning blocks;
- addressed multiple aspects of word knowledge;
- combined digital and traditional tools;
- asked learners to construct a personalised vocabulary-learning algorithm;
- used reflection journals, interviews and learner output.

Other faculty work emphasises that knowing a word includes meaning, form, pronunciation, grammatical behaviour and sociolinguistic connotation.

### Interpretation

DLIFLC vocabulary instruction appears to include **metacognitive strategy training** and learner personalisation, not only assigned lists.

### Claim status

**Strongly supported in documented interventions; not yet proven to be standard in all schools.**

---

## 6. Grammar instruction

The 2025 issue of *Dialog on Language Instruction* includes a process-based, learner-centred four-phase approach to grammar instruction using Mandarin examples.

This confirms that explicit grammar pedagogy exists within DLIFLC practice. It does not yet establish that the four-phase model is mandatory or institution-wide.

### Claim status

**Verified as a DLIFLC-published instructional model; scope currently context-specific.**

---

## 7. Recycling, remediation and special assistance

DLIFLC publications include work on:

- recycling lower-level material late in a course;
- supporting at-risk learners through Special Assistance;
- morale-building interventions;
- personalised materials;
- concept checking and instruction checking.

This indicates that the programme does not rely solely on a fixed forward-moving syllabus. It includes diagnostic correction and targeted support.

### Reconstructed system principle

> Progression is intensive, but not purely linear: weak foundations are revisited, individual deficits are identified, and extra assistance is layered onto the main course.

### Claim status

**Strongly supported as a system characteristic; operational details require further study.**

## 8. Preliminary lesson model

The following is a research synthesis, not an official DLIFLC template:

1. **Orientation or activation**  
   Establish topic, context, prior knowledge or communicative purpose.

2. **Target-language input**  
   Present spoken or written material with the target language as the main medium.

3. **Focused noticing or explicit instruction**  
   Direct attention to a grammatical, lexical, phonological, syntactic or cultural feature.

4. **Controlled processing practice**  
   Use chunking, mapping, decoding, concept-checking or structured rehearsal.

5. **Meaning-focused comprehension**  
   Reconnect the isolated feature to an authentic or semi-authentic message.

6. **Production**  
   Require spoken or written use rather than ending with recognition.

7. **Feedback and correction**  
   Diagnose misunderstanding, performance gaps and strategy failure.

8. **Recycling and homework**  
   Revisit material across later lessons and extend practice outside class.

9. **Targeted assistance where needed**  
   Add remediation or Special Assistance for learners not keeping pace.

### Confidence

**Moderate.** Each component is evidenced somewhere in DLIFLC publications, but the combined sequence is an analytical reconstruction.

---

## 9. Implications for a civilian model

The transferable element is not the ten-hour workload itself. A civilian adaptation should preserve the functional relationships:

- frequent contact with the language;
- target-language-primary instruction;
- explicit processing strategies;
- integration of listening, reading, speaking and writing;
- immediate production;
- feedback;
- cumulative recycling;
- diagnostic remediation;
- measurable proficiency outcomes.

The workload can be compressed, but removing these relationships would create a conventional self-study course rather than a DLI-inspired system.

## 10. Open questions

1. How are seven classroom hours divided across teachers, skills and activities?
2. How frequently are quizzes or progress tests administered?
3. How much new vocabulary is expected daily or weekly?
4. How are homework assignments corrected and recycled?
5. What triggers Special Assistance?
6. How does lesson structure differ among Japanese, Korean, Arabic and easier-category languages?
7. Which elements are formal policy and which are local school practice?
8. How are production tasks graded?
9. How closely do course materials align with DLPT task types?
10. What proportion of class time is teacher-led, pair work, individual work and technology-mediated practice?
