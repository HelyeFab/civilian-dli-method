## Initial verified institutional facts

The following are starting points, not yet a complete model:

1. DLIFLC publicly describes resident students as spending five days per week and seven hours per day in class, with approximately two to three hours of homework nightly.
2. Public descriptions place basic-course duration broadly between 36 and 64 weeks depending on language difficulty.
3. DLIFLC publishes a refereed practitioner journal, *Dialog on Language Instruction*, containing classroom research by institute faculty.
4. Recent institute publications document structured interventions in listening, vocabulary, grammar, special assistance and AI-supported personalisation.
5. DLIFLC's public GLOSS system provides proficiency-levelled lessons across modalities and topics, including Japanese.

Each fact must be entered into the claims register with exact source details before it is used in final synthesis.

## Early working hypothesis

DLIFLC success is unlikely to derive from a single secret technique. The emerging model is a system of interacting conditions and mechanisms:

- exceptional time-on-task;
- long programme duration;
- structured curriculum progression;
- high-frequency production and comprehension work;
- explicit strategy instruction;
- continuous feedback and assessment pressure;
- targeted remediation;
- expert instructor adaptation;
- authentic and proficiency-levelled materials;
- institutional accountability.

This remains a hypothesis and must not be promoted to a conclusion until tested against stronger evidence.
