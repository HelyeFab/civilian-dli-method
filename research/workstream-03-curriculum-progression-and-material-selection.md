# Workstream 03 — Curriculum Progression and Material Selection

## Research question

How does DLIFLC appear to move learners from beginner foundations toward authentic, unfamiliar, proficiency-level material, and what principles govern material selection as proficiency rises?

## Executive finding

The evidence does **not** support a simple model in which learners complete one fixed textbook sequence and then begin using authentic materials only at the end.

A more defensible reconstruction is:

1. establish sound, script, high-frequency forms, and controlled task language;
2. move into structured curriculum organised around language, culture, and functional tasks;
3. introduce authentic or near-authentic texts increasingly early, but with scaffolding appropriate to level;
4. broaden topic range and linguistic complexity as proficiency rises;
5. select later materials according to learner proficiency, diagnosed weaknesses, and target outcomes rather than textbook order alone;
6. continue recycling lower-level concepts during advanced work;
7. increase learner autonomy and responsibility for material choice over time.

This is a research reconstruction, not an official universal DLIFLC syllabus.

## 1. What is firmly established

### 1.1 DLIFLC distinguishes basic and advanced learning products

DLIFLC publicly separates its eLearning products into Basic Skills, Deployment, Cultural and Regional Studies, Advanced Skills, and Assessment.

Basic resources include Sound & Script and Military Tasks. Advanced resources such as GLOSS offer thousands of intermediate and advanced listening and reading lessons organised by topic and proficiency-building purpose.

**Inference:** the public product ecosystem reflects a progression from foundational decoding and task language toward topic-based work with more complex texts and listening passages.

**Confidence:** High for the existence of this progression in public products; Moderate for treating it as a complete mirror of the resident basic course.

### 1.2 DLIFLC produces most of its own materials

DLIFLC states that it produces most materials used in its basic and advanced courses.

**Implication:** curriculum design is institutionally controlled and can be adapted to proficiency targets, mission needs, and language-specific demands rather than relying entirely on commercial textbooks.

**Confidence:** High.

### 1.3 Authentic materials appear inside the course, not only after it

The 2021–2023 Linguist Next Arabic pilot reimagined the basic course in a digital environment. A participant reported that every passage used came from an authentic Arabic source. The pilot emphasised flexible digital delivery, higher-order analysis, autonomy, and data-informed personalisation.

This does **not** prove that all standard DLIFLC basic courses use authentic materials from the first day. It does prove that DLIFLC has implemented a full basic-course model in which authentic source material was central rather than reserved for the end.

**Confidence:** High for the pilot; Low if generalised to every language and school.

### 1.4 Advanced work does not abandon foundations

DLIFLC action research in Semester III Arabic documents an eight-week intervention that recycled lower-level concepts while integrating core curriculum and authentic materials across listening, reading, speaking, and review. Material selection and individualised instruction were based on learner needs and test performance.

**Implication:** progression is cumulative rather than purely linear. Advanced learners continue to revisit lower-level forms when these constrain proficiency.

**Confidence:** High for the documented Arabic intervention; Moderate as a broader design principle.

## 2. Open Architecture Curricular Design

A 2026 DLIFLC review states that Open Architecture Curricular Design (OACD) was institutionalised at DLIFLC in 2015 for high-beginner learners and above.

The review describes OACD as a response to limitations of lockstep textbook instruction. Its purpose is to structure learning experiences that are appropriate to learner level and needs while avoiding generic worksheets and rigid sequencing.

### What can be said safely

- OACD was institutionalised at DLIFLC.
- It applied from high-beginner level upward.
- It was designed to reduce lockstep dependence on textbooks.
- It prioritised material and activity selection according to learner level and need.

### What remains unverified

- the exact institution-wide design rules;
- whether every school implemented OACD identically;
- the proportion of teacher-created, centrally supplied, and authentic materials;
- the precise point at which learners entered OACD in each language;
- whether the 2015 implementation remains unchanged in 2026.

**Confidence:** High for institutionalisation and broad purpose; Moderate for current uniform implementation.

## 3. Reconstructed curriculum ladder

The following ladder synthesises official public products, DLIFLC faculty research, and the Linguist Next pilot.

### Stage 0 — Sound, script, and orientation

Likely priorities:

- phonological discrimination;
- sound-symbol mapping;
- writing system recognition;
- pronunciation routines;
- survival phrases;
- classroom language;
- cultural orientation.

Evidence includes DLIFLC's public Sound & Script materials and beginner products.

### Stage 1 — Controlled high-frequency language

Likely priorities:

- core sentence patterns;
- high-frequency vocabulary;
- basic interpersonal functions;
- short controlled listening and reading;
- heavily scaffolded production;
- rapid correction and repetition.

Evidence is indirect. Public beginner products support the broad outline, but the resident course's exact beginner lesson sequence is not publicly documented.

### Stage 2 — High-beginner transition

This is the earliest level at which OACD was reportedly institutionalised.

Likely changes:

- less rigid textbook sequencing;
- more teacher-selected materials;
- topic-based clusters;
- authentic excerpts or lightly adapted texts;
- greater variation by learner needs;
- explicit strategy training for listening, vocabulary, and reading.

### Stage 3 — Intermediate proficiency building

Likely priorities:

- longer authentic texts and audio;
- broader political, social, economic, and cultural topics;
- gist, detail, inference, and discourse organisation;
- paraphrase and summary;
- supported discussion;
- increasing tolerance for ambiguity;
- cumulative review of earlier language.

Public GLOSS materials and faculty research strongly support topic-based listening and reading development at this stage.

### Stage 4 — Advanced integration

Likely priorities:

- unfamiliar authentic material;
- dense news and analytical content;
- varied registers, accents, and dialects where relevant;
- extended speaking;
- cross-source synthesis;
- speed and endurance;
- concept knowledge needed to understand the content;
- targeted repair of persistent lower-level weaknesses.

Evidence includes advanced public materials, dialect resources, phone conversations, Semester III research, and proficiency-oriented assessment.

### Stage 5 — Operational transfer and autonomous maintenance

Likely priorities:

- independent material selection;
- diagnostic self-study;
- continued proficiency maintenance;
- mission-relevant domains;
- use of assessment feedback to select future study;
- self-regulated learning beyond graduation.

The Linguist Next pilot explicitly framed learner autonomy as preparation for post-graduation maintenance and enhancement.

## 4. Material-selection principles

The strongest evidence supports the following criteria.

### 4.1 Proficiency match

Material should be challenging enough to develop the next level of performance without making the task meaningless.

OACD's stated purpose was to provide experiences at the learner's level and tailored to need.

### 4.2 Diagnostic relevance

Material is selected partly in response to test performance and individual weaknesses.

The Semester III recycling study explicitly reports that material selection and individualised instruction were based on learner needs and test results.

### 4.3 Authenticity

Authentic source material has a significant role, especially at intermediate and advanced stages and in experimental redesigns.

Authenticity alone is not the method. It is paired with scaffolding, strategy instruction, recycling, and teacher mediation.

### 4.4 Topic breadth

The public DLIFLC ecosystem includes geography, history, economy, society, security, religion, traditions, urban and rural life, military tasks, regional dialects, and casual conversations.

**Inference:** topic breadth is necessary because proficiency tests measure ability across unfamiliar content, not mastery of one narrow curriculum domain.

### 4.5 Cultural and conceptual knowledge

Faculty reflection shows that learners may fail to understand a passage not because of language alone, but because they do not understand concepts such as tariffs, subsidies, or internships.

**Implication:** curriculum progression must build both linguistic competence and world/concept knowledge.

### 4.6 Modality balance

Later instruction appears to integrate listening, reading, speaking, vocabulary, grammar, and review rather than treating them as isolated courses.

### 4.7 Controlled recurrence

Older grammar, vocabulary, and discourse structures are intentionally recycled when they remain unstable.

### 4.8 Learner autonomy

As proficiency rises, learners appear to assume more responsibility for selecting tools, materials, and study priorities.

## 5. Authentic material is not the same as unsupported immersion

A recurring misconception is that DLI succeeds because learners are simply exposed to large volumes of native material.

The evidence instead supports this sequence:

1. choose or create material at an appropriate challenge level;
2. define the proficiency or processing objective;
3. pre-teach only what is necessary;
4. guide learners through listening or reading strategies;
5. require an observable performance;
6. provide feedback;
7. recycle linguistic weaknesses;
8. later retest with unfamiliar material.

Authentic material becomes effective because it is embedded in a diagnostic and instructional system.

## 6. Preliminary civilian adaptation

### Foundation phase

Use controlled material for pronunciation, script, high-frequency chunks, and basic sentence architecture.

### Transition phase

At high-beginner level, replace one fixed textbook path with a curated set of short topic units. Each unit should combine:

- one short authentic or near-authentic text;
- one listening passage;
- a small lexical set;
- one grammar or discourse feature;
- controlled production;
- cumulative retrieval.

### Intermediate phase

Use authentic material organised by topic and ILR/CEFR/JLPT difficulty. Train learners to extract gist, details, structure, stance, and implied meaning.

### Advanced phase

Increase speed, density, unfamiliarity, topic range, and register variation. Require summary, comparison, explanation, and spontaneous response.

### Diagnostic rule

Do not progress merely because a unit was completed. Progress when the learner can perform against new material at the intended level.

### Recycling rule

When a recurring lower-level weakness appears, create a short targeted intervention and then return the learner to advanced material.

## 7. Implications for Japanese

A Japanese adaptation should not equate progression with completing grammar chapters or memorising kanji lists.

A DLI-inspired Japanese ladder would include:

1. kana, mora timing, pitch awareness, and core classroom chunks;
2. high-frequency sentence patterns and controlled dialogues;
3. early short authentic artefacts such as signs, menus, messages, announcements, and simple social posts;
4. progressive listening from careful speech toward natural contractions and connected speech;
5. reading expansion from sentence-level material to notices, narratives, news, commentary, and public-information texts;
6. continuous recycling of particles, verb forms, counters, register, and discourse markers;
7. concept and cultural knowledge needed to understand Japanese institutions and public discourse;
8. proficiency checks using unfamiliar material rather than only JLPT-style recognition questions.

Kanji should be integrated into words, collocations, sentence patterns, and authentic texts rather than taught as an isolated inventory.

**Claim:** Later-stage DLIFLC instruction can select material according to learner needs and test performance while recycling foundational concepts.

**Status:** Verified in a Semester III Arabic action-research intervention.

**Confidence:** High for the documented intervention.

### Claim C03-05

**Claim:** Curriculum progression at DLI is cumulative and diagnostic rather than strictly linear.

**Status:** Strongly supported synthesis.

**Confidence:** Moderate to High.

## 9. Unresolved questions

- What exact percentage of basic-course content is authentic, adapted, or textbook-based by language?
- How are texts assigned ILR difficulty before use?
- What are the formal design components of OACD?
- How much freedom does an individual instructor have to replace core materials?
- How are topic domains distributed across semesters?
- How do Japanese, Korean, Arabic, Chinese, and shorter Category I/II courses differ?
- Are authentic materials introduced at the same point in every school?
- How are vocabulary load and grammar complexity controlled in authentic texts?
- How are learner-selected materials reviewed for level and quality?
- How does culture instruction connect to language assessment?
