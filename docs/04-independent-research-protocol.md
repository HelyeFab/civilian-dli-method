# Independent Research Protocol

## Purpose

This document defines the assistant-led research stream used to reconstruct DLIFLC instructional practice independently from the user's own research. The two streams must remain separate until a deliberate comparison stage.

## Independence rule

Until comparison begins:

- do not use the user's findings as evidence;
- do not merge the user's hypotheses into the assistant research model;
- record source provenance for every substantive claim;
- distinguish direct evidence from synthesis and inference;
- preserve contradictory evidence rather than smoothing it away.

## Research question

What repeatable instructional mechanisms appear to explain how DLIFLC develops high levels of language proficiency under intensive conditions, and which of those mechanisms can be adapted responsibly for civilian Japanese self-study?

## Parallel evidence tracks

### Track A — Institutional structure

Investigate course length, daily schedule, homework load, staffing, class organisation, faculty development, support hours, immersion and assessment requirements.

### Track B — Lesson architecture

Investigate how lessons introduce, model, practise, retrieve, recycle and assess language across speaking, listening, reading, writing, grammar and vocabulary.

### Track C — Skill development

Investigate separate progressions for listening, speaking, reading, writing, vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation and cultural competence.

### Track D — Assessment and remediation

Investigate the DLPT/OPI/ILR relationship, internal testing, feedback loops, special assistance, at-risk learner support and remediation.

### Track E — Learner experience

Collect identifiable student and instructor accounts to test whether official descriptions match lived classroom practice.

### Track F — Transferability

Classify mechanisms as:

- directly transferable;
- transferable with reduced intensity;
- dependent on expert instruction;
- dependent on cohort or institutional conditions;
- military-specific;
- unsuitable for civilian replication.

### Track G — Japanese adaptation

Test all candidate mechanisms against Japanese-specific requirements: kana, kanji, orthographic depth, pitch accent, register, politeness, sentence-final processing, listening segmentation and literacy progression.

## Evidence workflow

1. Locate primary or near-primary sources.
2. Create source notes using the repository template.
3. Extract atomic claims rather than broad summaries.
4. Enter claims in the claims register.
5. Seek at least one independent corroborating source for important claims.
6. Record scope, date, language programme and learner level.
7. Mark conflicts explicitly.
8. Only then synthesize a working model.

## Source priority

1. DLIFLC policy, official pages, handbooks and course artefacts.
2. DLIFLC faculty research and refereed journals.
3. DoD, Army, GAO, RAND, FSI and other government research.
4. Peer-reviewed independent research.
5. Identifiable former students and instructors.
6. Anonymous accounts used only as leads or corroboration.

## Output sequence

1. Evidence map.
2. Reconstructed day and week.
3. Reconstructed lesson cycle.
4. Skill-specific mechanisms.
5. Assessment and remediation model.
6. Transferability matrix.
7. Civilian DLI framework.
8. Japanese-specific implementation.
9. Comparison with the user's independent findings.

## Comparison protocol

At comparison time, findings will be grouped as:

- agreement;
- complementary findings;
- apparent disagreement;
- different scope or context;
- unsupported claim;
- unresolved question.

No side will be treated as correct merely because it found more sources. Source quality, scope and explanatory strength will determine confidence.
