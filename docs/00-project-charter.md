# Project Charter

## Purpose

This project will produce an evidence-based reconstruction of the Defense Language Institute's language-training system and translate its most transferable mechanisms into a civilian learning framework.

## Core research question

**How does DLI organise instruction, practice, assessment, and review to produce usable language proficiency under intensive time constraints, and which parts can be reproduced by a civilian learner?**

## Secondary questions

1. What does a typical student day and week look like?
2. How are new language items introduced, practised, tested, and recycled?
3. How are listening, speaking, reading, writing, grammar, vocabulary, and culture integrated?
4. How much of DLI's effectiveness comes from pedagogy versus total study time, selection, pressure, and institutional support?
5. What varies by language, course generation, school, instructor, and historical period?
6. Which practices are supported by broader language-acquisition research?
7. What would a sustainable civilian implementation look like?
8. What changes are necessary for Japanese?

## Scope

### Included

- DLIFLC resident basic courses and their instructional environment.
- Official descriptions of curriculum, assessment, faculty development, and proficiency goals.
- Credible accounts from instructors, graduates, and students.
- ILR-related assessment and outcomes.
- Comparison with relevant intensive language programmes and research.
- Civilian and Japanese-specific adaptation.

### Excluded or treated cautiously

- Classified operational training.
- Claims based solely on promotional language.
- A single graduate's experience presented as universal.
- Unsupported claims about exact vocabulary counts, pass rates, or teaching sequences.
- The assumption that every DLI language school or course operates identically.

## Deliverables

- Source catalogue.
- Claims register.
- Reconstructed model of DLI instruction.
- Civilian implementation handbook.
- Japanese-specific implementation.
- Sample schedules, lesson templates, drills, and assessment checkpoints.

## Research principles

- Primary sources first.
- Date every source and claim where historical change matters.
- Distinguish description from interpretation.
- Preserve disagreements between credible sources.
- Label uncertainty explicitly.
- Do not turn repeated anecdotes into verified facts without corroboration.
