# Source Evaluation Framework

## Objective

This framework determines how much weight a source may carry when reconstructing DLIFLC practice. Credibility and usefulness are related but separate: an official source may be highly credible about programme length yet reveal little about daily pedagogy, while a detailed student account may be useful but unable to establish institute-wide practice.

## Source tiers

### Tier A — Primary institutional and assessment sources

Examples:

- current DLIFLC programme, academic and registrar pages;
- official student handbooks, catalogues, policy documents and course descriptions;
- official Department of Defense or U.S. Army publications;
- official ILR Skill Level Descriptions;
- public DLIFLC courseware and assessment documentation.

Best for:

- institutional structure;
- course duration;
- formal outcomes;
- eligibility;
- assessment frameworks;
- stated programme design.

Limitations:

- may be promotional;
- may describe intended rather than enacted practice;
- often omits classroom detail.

### Tier B — DLIFLC faculty research and professional publications

Examples:

- *Dialog on Language Instruction*;
- *Applied Language Learning*;
- presentations or papers authored by identifiable DLIFLC faculty;
- documented action research from a named school or course.

Best for:

- instructional activities;
- classroom problems and interventions;
- teacher development;
- learner support;
- evidence of practices in specific settings.

Limitations:

- a published intervention may be local rather than standard;
- action research may use small samples;
- publication implies relevance, not universal adoption.

### Tier C — Independent scholarly and oversight sources

Examples:

- peer-reviewed second-language-acquisition research;
- government audits and congressional reports;
- accreditation reviews;
- academic studies of intensive government language programmes.

Best for:

- external validation;
- outcomes and institutional constraints;
- comparison with other programmes;
- testing assumptions about transferability.

Limitations:

- may study historical versions of the programme;
- general language-learning findings do not prove DLIFLC usage.

### Tier D — Identifiable instructor or graduate accounts

Examples:

- named interviews;
- published memoirs;
- detailed talks, podcasts or articles;
- accounts with language, year, course and role identified.

Best for:

- daily routine;
- lived classroom experience;
- workload and learner behaviour;
- generating questions for verification.

Limitations:

- memory and selection bias;
- variation by language, teacher, service and era;
- cannot alone establish institute-wide policy.

### Tier E — Anonymous or weakly attributable accounts

Examples:

- Reddit posts;
- forums;
- anonymous comments;
- unsourced summaries and commercial blogs.

Best for:

- discovering vocabulary, disputed claims or leads;
- identifying issues that recur across accounts.

Limitations:

- identity and experience cannot be verified;
- high risk of repetition, exaggeration and outdated information;
- never sufficient as the sole support for a major claim.

## Evaluation dimensions

Score each source from 0 to 3 on each dimension.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Authority | Unknown | Relevant personal experience | Qualified author | Responsible institution or recognised specialist |
| Proximity | Third-hand | General commentary | Direct participant or analysis | Direct policy, data, observation or artefact |
| Specificity | Vague | Some context | Language/course/time identified | Fully bounded population, method and setting |
| Currency | Clearly outdated | Date uncertain | Recent enough for question | Current or explicitly historical |
| Transparency | No method/evidence | Minimal support | Sources or method partly explained | Evidence and limitations clearly reported |
| Corroboration | Contradicted/isolated | Uncorroborated | Consistent with another source | Independently supported by multiple strong sources |

The numerical score helps comparison but never overrides judgement.

## Claim-status vocabulary

Every material claim should receive one status:

- **Verified** — directly supported by authoritative primary evidence and, where necessary, current.
- **Strongly supported** — supported by multiple credible sources with no material contradiction.
- **Supported in a bounded context** — demonstrated for a specific school, language, class or intervention.
- **Anecdotal** — reported by one or more participants but not independently established.
- **Inferred** — reasoned from evidence but not directly stated or observed.
- **Contested** — credible sources disagree or practice clearly varies.
- **Unverified** — plausible but presently unsupported.
- **Disconfirmed** — reliable evidence shows the claim is false or materially misleading.

## Claim scope

No claim may be broader than its evidence. Use scope labels:

- institute-wide;
- school-wide;
- language-specific;
- course-specific;
- classroom-specific;
- historical period;
- individual experience.

For example, a Mandarin instructor’s action-research paper can support “this intervention was used in the reported Mandarin class”, but not automatically “DLI teaches grammar this way”.

## Handling official claims

Official descriptions should be recorded as either:

- **formal design** — what the institution says should happen;
- **operational fact** — verifiable structure, duration or requirement;
- **promotional characterisation** — broad claims such as “elite”, “immersive” or “world-class”.

Promotional language is not evidence of effectiveness.

## Handling outcomes

Outcome claims must specify:

- test and version where known;
- skill tested;
- target score;
- population;
- completion versus pass rate;
- date;
- whether speaking was assessed separately;
- whether attrition changes the interpretation.

## Conflict protocol

When sources conflict:

1. check whether they concern different years, languages or services;
2. distinguish policy from classroom implementation;
3. prefer direct and current evidence for current factual questions;
4. retain both accounts when variation is plausible;
5. mark the claim `Contested` until resolved.

## Citation practice

Repository documents should link to the source and include author, title, institution, year and access date when practical. Verbatim quotations should be short and used only when exact wording matters. Most evidence should be paraphrased with scope preserved.
