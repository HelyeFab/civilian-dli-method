# Research Methodology

## Purpose

This project aims to reconstruct how the Defense Language Institute Foreign Language Center (DLIFLC) actually teaches, then translate the most defensible elements into a civilian self-study system.

The project does **not** assume that every practice used at DLIFLC is optimal, transferable, or publicly documented. It distinguishes:

1. what DLIFLC officially states;
2. what DLIFLC faculty publish about classroom practice;
3. what independent research supports;
4. what former students and instructors report;
5. what the project infers from several sources.

## Core research questions

The research asks:

- How are basic language programmes organised?
- What happens during a normal instructional day and week?
- How are listening, reading, speaking, writing, grammar, vocabulary, culture and operational content integrated?
- How are new items introduced, practised, recycled and assessed?
- How does instruction change across proficiency levels?
- What support is provided to struggling or advanced students?
- How strongly do programme assessments shape classroom practice?
- Which mechanisms can be reproduced by an independent civilian learner?
- Which mechanisms depend on institutional scale, military discipline, selective admissions or multiple instructors?

## Unit of analysis

The project will not treat “the DLI method” as a single fixed teaching recipe. The unit of analysis will be a **documented instructional mechanism**, such as:

- target-language classroom interaction;
- bottom-up listening work;
- process-based grammar instruction;
- vocabulary strategy training;
- cultural immersion;
- proficiency-oriented assessment;
- special-assistance instruction;
- structured recycling;
- instructor teams and faculty standardisation.

Each mechanism must be documented separately before being assembled into a larger model.

## Evidence workflow

For every important source:

1. create a source note using `templates/source-note-template.md`;
2. record the author, institution, date, source type and URL;
3. extract only claims the source can directly support;
4. note population, language, course level and instructional setting;
5. record limitations and possible institutional bias;
6. add relevant claims to `research/claims-register.md`;
7. seek corroboration before treating the claim as general DLIFLC practice.

## Triangulation rule

A claim about current, institute-wide classroom practice should normally require at least one of the following:

- a current official DLIFLC policy or programme description plus a DLIFLC faculty publication;
- two independent DLIFLC faculty publications from different schools or languages;
- an official source plus consistent accounts from several former students or instructors;
- a primary institutional source supported by direct observation material, course artefacts or public learning products.

A single personal account may establish that a practice occurred, but not that it is universal.

## Time sensitivity

DLIFLC changes curricula, course lengths, languages, tests and institutional structures. Every factual statement should therefore include a date or source date where practical.

The repository will distinguish:

- **current practice** — supported by recent sources;
- **historical practice** — accurately documented but not assumed current;
- **persistent practice** — found across multiple time periods;
- **unknown current status** — historically documented but not recently verified.

## Analysis stages

### Stage 1 — Institutional map

Document programme lengths, schools, student population, daily instructional load, faculty organisation, assessment framework and immersion resources.

### Stage 2 — Instructional mechanisms

Extract evidence on lesson design, target-language use, grammar, vocabulary, listening, reading, speaking, writing, culture, review and learner support.

### Stage 3 — System model

Map the interaction among:

- inputs;
- guided practice;
- independent practice;
- feedback;
- retrieval and recycling;
- assessment;
- remediation;
- immersion;
- learner autonomy.

### Stage 4 — External validation

Compare each proposed mechanism with credible second-language-acquisition research. Agreement with research strengthens transferability but does not prove DLIFLC uses the mechanism institute-wide.

### Stage 5 — Civilian adaptation

For each mechanism, identify:

- the function it serves;
- the minimum viable civilian version;
- required time and materials;
- dependencies on teachers or peers;
- likely risks, including burnout and false intensity;
- adaptations for Japanese.

## Interpretation rules

- Do not equate long study hours with an instructional method.
- Do not equate military discipline with language pedagogy.
- Do not infer a universal practice from one language school.
- Do not assume successful graduates prove every component is effective.
- Do not convert a faculty recommendation into evidence of institute-wide adoption.
- Do not describe an inference as an observed fact.
- Preserve contradictions rather than smoothing them away.

## Reproducibility

Every synthesis document should link back to source notes and claim identifiers. Another researcher should be able to trace a conclusion from the final model to the evidence used to build it.
