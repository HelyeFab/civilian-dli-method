# Questions to Investigate

This document is the research backlog. Questions are grouped by system component rather than by source.

## 1. Institutional structure

- What is common across all basic courses, and what is controlled by individual schools or departments?
- How many instructors normally work with one class?
- How are teaching teams organised and rotated?
- What is the relationship between academic leadership and military service units?
- How much discretion do individual instructors have?
- What does DLIFLC mean by “student-centred”, “whole-person” and “culturally based” instruction?
- How has the system changed since major curriculum reforms?

## 2. Learners and selection

- How are students selected and assigned to languages?
- What role do aptitude tests play?
- What prior language-learning experience do students have?
- How do age, service, motivation and career consequences affect results?
- What are attrition, recycling and reassignment processes?
- Which learner characteristics limit comparison with voluntary civilian study?

## 3. Course architecture

- How are the 36-, 48- and 64-week programmes divided into semesters, phases or modules?
- What proficiency targets apply at intermediate checkpoints and graduation?
- How quickly does content difficulty increase?
- How are general language, cultural studies and operational language sequenced?
- Is the curriculum textbook-led, open-architecture, task-based or a mixture?
- What is fixed nationally and what is language-specific?

## 4. Daily and weekly rhythm

- What happens in each instructional hour?
- Is the seventh hour always normal instruction, remediation or special assistance?
- How much time is spent on each skill?
- How much homework is assigned, and what forms does it take?
- How frequently are quizzes, reviews and larger assessments used?
- How do physical training and military duties affect study and recovery?
- Are weekends used for mandatory or voluntary study?

## 5. Lesson architecture

- How is a new lesson opened?
- How are objectives communicated?
- How is prior material retrieved?
- How are new words, structures and cultural concepts introduced?
- What ratio of modelling, controlled practice and freer production is typical?
- How is understanding checked?
- How is corrective feedback delivered?
- How does the lesson close, and how is follow-up assigned?

## 6. Target-language use

- At what point does target-language instruction begin?
- Is there a formal target-language percentage?
- When is English intentionally used?
- How do teachers make low-level target-language instruction comprehensible?
- How is target-language use balanced against accurate explanation?
- Does practice differ by language category or writing system?

## 7. Vocabulary

- How many lexical items are introduced in a typical day or week?
- Are words taught individually, in chunks, by theme or through texts?
- What vocabulary-learning strategies are explicitly taught?
- How are receptive and productive mastery distinguished?
- How are words recycled across listening, reading, speaking and writing?
- Are flashcards or spaced-repetition systems officially used?
- How are high-frequency, academic, cultural and operational vocabularies balanced?

## 8. Grammar

- Is grammar introduced deductively, inductively or through a staged process?
- How much terminology is used?
- How are comprehension, memorisation, internalisation and application handled?
- How are grammar errors prioritised in feedback?
- How are forms revisited after their initial lesson?
- How does grammar instruction change at higher proficiency?

## 9. Listening

- How is bottom-up decoding taught?
- How are authentic speed, accents and noise introduced?
- Are transcripts used before, during or after listening?
- How are intensive and extensive listening balanced?
- What activity sequence surrounds an audio passage?
- How are listening failures diagnosed?
- How closely do classroom listening tasks resemble the DLPT?

## 10. Reading

- How are new writing systems taught?
- When do students begin authentic texts?
- How are intensive, extensive and timed reading used?
- How are unknown words handled?
- Are read-aloud activities used for decoding and fluency?
- How are genre, inference, tone and cultural knowledge developed?
- How closely do classroom reading tasks resemble the DLPT?

## 11. Speaking

- How much individual speaking time does each learner receive?
- Which drills are used for pronunciation, automaticity and interaction?
- How are role-plays, presentations, debates and interviews sequenced?
- How are fluency and accuracy balanced?
- How is speaking assessed relative to listening and reading?
- What role do native-speaker instructors and off-site immersion play?

## 12. Writing

- When does composition begin?
- How are script accuracy, sentence construction and extended discourse developed?
- Is writing primarily a learning tool, an assessed skill or both?
- How is feedback delivered and revised?
- How much keyboard versus handwritten production is required?

## 13. Pronunciation and prosody

- Is pronunciation explicitly taught beyond the opening phase?
- What use is made of phonetics, minimal pairs, shadowing, recording and visual feedback?
- How are rhythm, intonation, stress, tones or pitch accent addressed?
- What standards are expected for intelligibility and sociolinguistic appropriateness?

## 14. Culture and area studies

- How is cultural knowledge integrated into language lessons?
- Which cultural domains are taught explicitly?
- How are contested, political or unfamiliar concepts handled?
- How is intercultural competence assessed?
- What is the relationship among cultural activities, field trips, immersion and proficiency?

## 15. Assessment

- Which tests are teacher-created, school-created or institute-wide?
- How often are formative and summative assessments administered?
- How are ILR levels operationalised in curriculum and feedback?
- What does the DLPT test, and what does it omit?
- How do test targets influence classroom content?
- How are speaking scores obtained?
- What are the consequences of failing checkpoints or graduation standards?

## 16. Feedback and remediation

- What is Special Assistance, and who receives it?
- How are individual learning problems diagnosed?
- What interventions are used for vocabulary, listening, grammar and motivation?
- How are students recycled into later cohorts?
- How are high-performing learners extended?
- What evidence exists for the effectiveness of remediation?

## 17. Faculty development and quality control

- What is included in the Common Faculty Development Instructor Course?
- What is included in the DLIFLC Instructor Certification Course?
- How are new instructors mentored and observed?
- How are classroom standards calibrated across instructors?
- What role do action research, professional learning and student feedback play?
- How are native-speaker expertise and formal teaching skill combined?

## 18. Technology and materials

- Which learning-management, authoring and assessment systems are used?
- How are audio, video, corpora, transcription and adaptive tools incorporated?
- What can public products such as GLOSS and Headstart2 reveal about instructional design?
- How are generative-AI tools currently being piloted or governed?
- Which technology is essential and which merely supports the underlying pedagogy?

## 19. Intensity, retention and wellbeing

- Which benefits come from total hours rather than a distinctive method?
- How much sleep and recovery do successful students obtain?
- What are the cognitive and emotional costs of the programme?
- How do anxiety, motivation and military consequences influence performance?
- Which intensity level is sustainable for civilians?
- How should a civilian version avoid burnout and shallow cramming?

## 20. Japanese-specific adaptation

- How does the Japanese course allocate time among listening, speaking, kana and kanji?
- How are readings, compounds and script production sequenced?
- How are politeness, register, gendered language and sociolinguistic appropriateness taught?
- Is pitch accent addressed explicitly?
- How is authentic Japanese introduced before full kanji coverage?
- Which operational-language content should be replaced for civilians?
- How should DLI mechanisms map to JLPT goals without becoming test-only study?

## 21. Civilian reconstruction

For every verified mechanism:

- What problem does it solve?
- What is the smallest effective implementation?
- Can one learner perform it alone?
- Does it require a tutor, partner or native speaker?
- Can AI simulate part of the interaction without creating false feedback?
- What weekly time commitment is realistic?
- How should progress be assessed?
- What evidence would show that the civilian adaptation works?
